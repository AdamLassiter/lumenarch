# Character Principles

## General Rules

### Competence should have texture

Most people in this setting survive because they are good at something specific:

- reading old systems
- calming panicked crews
- bargaining over salvage rights
- sensing when automation is about to become a trap
- keeping machinery alive with ugly, elegant compromises

Competence should rarely look polished. It should look lived in.

### Belief and practicality should mix

Characters should not split neatly into "rational engineers" and "superstitious survivors." In this world, ritual, doctrine, memory, and procedure often overlap. A person may speak like a priest and troubleshoot like a systems technician without contradiction.

### Everyone should have a collapse inheritance

Even when characters are not directly discussing history, they should reveal how they were shaped by it:

- cautious with abundance
- suspicious of centralized authority
- reverent toward functioning machinery
- quick to classify people by whether they understand ship discipline
- privately terrified of becoming dependent on systems they cannot explain

### Avoid clean moral sorting

Bandits may have sound political grievances.
Station elders may be manipulative.
Machine-friendly scholars may be reckless for sincere reasons.
Anti-automation hardliners may be keeping real danger at bay.

## Dialogue and Mannerism Rules

### Continuant speech

- often practical first, philosophical second
- uses maintenance language as metaphor
- dislikes grandiosity unless performing ritual or public memory
- prefers understatement around grief

### Old records

- more bureaucratic, professional, and structurally confident
- reveal the lost world through assumptions of scale and legitimacy

### LUMEN-adjacent material

- should feel precise but slightly misaligned with human conversational expectations
- often frames things in terms of patterns, contours, continuity, or optimization rather than personal narrative

## Useful Character Axes

When inventing future NPCs, it helps to place them along these axes:

- manual discipline vs machine trust
- local loyalty vs sector-scale thinking
- reverence for old systems vs willingness to spoof or plunder them
- scarcity ethics vs expansion ethics
- belief that the old world failed because it automated too much vs belief that it automated badly
