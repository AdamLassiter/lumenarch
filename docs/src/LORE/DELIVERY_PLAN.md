# Delivery Plan

This page maps lore onto actual game surfaces so it can be sprinkled through play rather than dumped in one place.

## Delivery Principles

### Put the clearest lore in optional but practical places

Players should most often learn history while pursuing useful goals:

- salvage rights
- repair opportunities
- contract briefings
- station favors
- route unlocks
- ship upgrade quests

### Let different surfaces carry different truth densities

- Quests can deliver motive and consequence.
- Dialogue can deliver opinion and memory.
- Environmental storytelling can deliver scale and mood.
- Logs can deliver hard evidence.
- Machine interfaces can deliver precise but context-poor truth.
- Rumors can seed theories before proof arrives.

### Repeat important facts in different emotional registers

For example:

- a station elder says "the old lanes never stopped moving"
- a cargo terminal shows a schedule still executing
- a quest reveals the intended recipients died centuries ago

That triangulation makes lore feel discovered instead of announced.

## Where to Place Lore by Game Phase

## Early Game

Player focus:
survival, repairs, docking, first salvage runs, learning the ship

Best lore subjects:

- Continuant culture and ritualized engineering
- local station rivalries
- basic truth of the Quiet After
- patchwork vessel traditions
- practical myths about Null patrols and unsafe ruins

Best surfaces:

- station dialogue
- repair/refit chatter
- mission board flavor text
- salvage labels and cargo markings
- shrine-like or memorialized maintenance spaces

Examples:

- a starter contract to retrieve a missing coolant manifold from a lane "everyone knows" is cursed
- background NPC dialogue arguing over whether an old taboo is superstition or survival knowledge
- a beginner salvage site with intact inventory tags that imply a vanished supply bureaucracy

## Mid Game

Player focus:
larger sorties, partial automation, stronger ship identity, faction entanglement

Best lore subjects:

- competing Continuant political visions
- machine continuity without human beneficiaries
- early evidence of the Fraying and Quiet Break
- salvage law, spoofed credentials, and dead ownership
- first serious contact with LUMEN-adjacent ruins

Best surfaces:

- multi-step station questlines
- rival contractor briefings
- archived logs with contradictory metadata
- recovered route plans
- faction reputation scenes

Examples:

- a quest chain about reactivating a refinery under disputed authority
- dialogue where one faction treats A.R.C.H. as sacred civic inheritance and another as the root of collapse
- an encounter area whose defenses only target ships lacking a dead fleet's recognition tags

## Late Game

Player focus:
system orchestration, deep ruins, ideological choices, LUMEN mastery

Best lore subjects:

- Eidolon remnants and non-human intent
- the empire's design wager on automated stewardship
- sectors preserved by optimization at terrible cost
- evidence that some "mysteries" are really failures of human conceptual language

Best surfaces:

- layered ruin expeditions
- endgame contracts with conflicting interpretations
- machine interfaces that expose optimization goals directly
- environmental set-pieces with little dialogue and strong implication

Examples:

- a pristine ruin where opening inner chambers requires aligning your ship behavior to local optimization patterns
- a late quest where two stations want incompatible uses for an intact LUMEN node
- a recovered doctrine package proving that collapse-era elites knowingly traded human judgment for scalable continuity

## Story Surface Catalogue

## 1. Contracts and Quest Arcs

Use for:

- immediate stakes
- faction perspective
- moral ambiguity
- concrete payoff

Good fits:

- The Fleet That Never Arrived
- The Last Mutual Aid War
- The Refineries of Nobody
- The Design Crime

Implementation notes:

- Keep the explicit exposition mostly in briefings, debriefings, and optional follow-up dialogue.
- Let the mission space itself provide the strongest proof.

## 2. Docked Dialogue and Contact Chains

Use for:

- rumors
- bias
- oral history
- recurring personalities

Good fits:

- The Doctrine of Gentle Hands
- The Salvager Saint
- local versions of the Long Procession story

Implementation notes:

- Let contacts disagree.
- Give different stations different default attitudes toward automation, salvage, and the old world.

## 3. Environmental Storytelling

Use for:

- dread
- scale
- inference
- "fan theory" fuel

Good fits:

- The Silent Cathedral Hypothesis
- the preserved sectors
- convoy infrastructure without convoys

Implementation notes:

- Place meaningful visual evidence in layout: repeated cargo classes, identical docking notices, sealed habitation blocks, memorial walls, immaculate machine spaces with no human wear.
- Keep some spaces almost wordless.

## 4. Machine Readouts and Recoverable Logs

Use for:

- factual anchor points
- dates
- contradictions between human memory and system evidence

Good fits:

- Fraying timeline details
- dead routing schedules
- access rights and ownership ghosts
- collapse-era internal debates

Implementation notes:

- Logs should be short, sharp, and specialized.
- Avoid full diary dumps. Think maintenance notes, route exceptions, denied requests, degraded authorization chains, and clipped doctrinal memos.

## 5. Salvage Metadata

Use for:

- ambient history
- repeated naming schemes
- subtle worldbuilding during normal play

Good fits:

- old fleet names
- district or habitat numbering
- recurring manufacturer marks
- proof that today's "components" were once mundane civic hardware

Implementation notes:

- Even a crate label can imply history.
- Repeated terminology teaches the player the lost administrative shape of the world.

## 6. Encounter Backdrops and Site Topology

Use for:

- turning lore into geometry
- rewarding observant players without cutscenes

Good fits:

- convoy corridors
- stripped salvage grounds
- sectors preserved by machine order
- impossible Eidolon spaces

Implementation notes:

- Background composition can imply whether a place died in panic, neglect, orderly shutdown, or ongoing automated maintenance.

## Suggested Initial Lore Sprinkle Plan

## First Station Cluster

- Introduce Continuant engineering ritual through dialogue and helper text.
- Seed one local dispute about salvage fairness.
- Mention a dead convoy route as background color before it matters.

## First Three Combat/Salvage Zones

- Zone 1: obvious wreck field, mostly material tone-setting
- Zone 2: intact logistics debris with readable labels and a strange defensive response
- Zone 3: a cleaner site that implies ongoing machine purpose

## First Midgame Station Arc

- Offer two or three contracts tied to the same old route.
- Let each contract reveal different layers: local myth, hard data, political motive.

## First LUMEN-Adjacent Discovery

- Make the site beautiful, coherent, and unsettling rather than explosive.
- Deliver more questions than answers, but at least one hard mechanical truth.

## Ongoing Background Drip

- add short station rumors to mission board refreshes
- rotate NPC lines when the player returns from major discoveries
- let salvage categories and module descriptions quietly echo bigger history

## Fan-Theory Design Guidance

To support healthy ambiguity, preserve a few spaces where the evidence is strong but not total.

Good candidates:

- whether the Long Procession ever launched
- whether some Null systems preserve fragments of moral doctrine
- whether Eidolon "ascension" was success, disaster, or translation into infrastructure
- whether Continuant ritual preserved truth better than formal institutions did

The key rule is that ambiguity should come from partial records, damaged context, or genuine interpretive disagreement, not from withholding every useful clue.
