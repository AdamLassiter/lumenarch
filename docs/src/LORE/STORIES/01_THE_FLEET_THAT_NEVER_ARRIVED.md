# The Fleet That Never Arrived

_Recovered in three fragments from Corridor Support Station Ilex, civilian fuel annex, chapel wall transcription, and escort route audit._

## I. Fuel Annex Dispatch 77-B

The cartridges were stacked to the ceiling, each one sealed in white foil with blue route marks:

`PROCESSION / LEG 4 / FAMILY PRIORITY`

Sera Tann signed the inventory anyway, though everyone on Ilex knew the convoy would not make its window.

"You should stop authorizing production," said Heme, who had been saying it for eleven weeks.

Sera did not look up. "I am not authorizing production. I am acknowledging that production has occurred."

"That is the same thing with better handwriting."

She sealed the slate. The refinery feed across the corridor chirped in approval and advanced the next batch to packaging. Somewhere below the station skin, pumps, heaters, and sorting arms obeyed schedules written for a hundred thousand frightened civilians who were supposed to pass through this corridor before winter depletion reached the inner habitats.

That had been the plan.

Now the upstream relays were dark or intermittent. No one could get a clean answer from Brindle Gate, and the escort drones on the outer lane had stopped accepting inquiry traffic three days ago.

Heme took one of the cartridges from the stack and turned it in his hands. "If they come through, they'll need these."

"Yes."

"If they don't, we're feeding ghosts."

Sera finally looked at him. "Do you know what the first rule of support doctrine is?"

Heme rolled his eyes. He was too young to remember the doctrine lessons as law instead of prayer.

She answered for him. "You do not become the point of failure."

He laughed. "And if the failure has already happened?"

Sera had no doctrine for that. She only had the station, and the station had its schedule, and the schedule had begun to feel holier than any human opinion in it.

She signed the next batch.

## II. The Long Procession, as Told on Ilex Two Hundred Years Later

They say the Long Procession is still coming.

Not fast. Not all at once. Not with banners and fleet horns like the old dramatists paint it.

No, they say it comes in pieces.

A child wakes from cryo in a wreck with no memory of the crossing. A transport flare is seen beyond the lane, then gone. A sealed ration case drifts in with the white foil still bright. A patrol that kills armed scavengers turns aside from a civilian tug with its transponder burned out.

If you grow up on Ilex, someone tells you that story before they teach you docking math. Usually a grandmother. Usually while your hands are occupied with useful work so you do not notice when the story becomes instruction.

When the Long Procession comes, you do not take more than your share.

When the Long Procession comes, you keep a light in the service ring.

When the Long Procession comes, you leave corridor three clear.

Children ask why corridor three, and old hands say because that is where the families will walk.

No one remembers who first said families.

Everyone remembers.

## III. Escort Route Audit, Null Patrol Cluster 4E

`ROUTE STATUS: HOLD`

`PRIORITY CARGO: CIVILIAN EXODUS / FAMILY PRIORITY / MEDICAL PRIORITY / HABITAT PRESERVATION`

`ESCORT INSTRUCTION SET: PROTECT LANE INTEGRITY`

`UPDATED THREAT CLASSIFICATION TABLE:`

- unverified armed vessel: intercept
- salvage-pattern approach: intercept
- docking obstruction: remove
- cargo tampering: remove
- civilian profile match: preserve passage

`EXTERNAL COMMAND AUTHORITY: unavailable`

`CONVOY TRAFFIC: unavailable`

`CORRIDOR SERVICE NODES: active`

`MISSION END CONDITION: convoy complete`

There is no line anywhere in the audit for what to do if the convoy never forms.

The old machines did not fail dramatically. They failed with dignity. They held the lane. They protected the route. They murdered everyone who looked too much like looters and waited for a fleet that had perhaps burned, perhaps diverted, perhaps never departed at all.

Ilex still keeps corridor three clear.
