# The Refineries of Nobody

_First-person account attributed to credential broker Aven Rusk, copied from a debt deposition on Cinder Dock._

People like to call me a forger because it is a simpler word than historian.

If I were a forger, I would invent authority. What I actually do is find out which corpses the machines still respect.

The refinery at Sel Edge looks like every refinery dream ever told to a hungry mechanic. Clean rails. Quiet lights. Intake arms polished by use instead of weather. You can feed it mixed scrap and tagged ore, and six hours later it will offer you perfect repair laminates sealed in old civic packaging, ready to slot into ships built three hundred years after the offices that ordered them stopped breathing.

There is one catch.

Sel Edge does not make anything for you.

It makes things for Municipal Recovery Cohort Nineteen, Habitat Cluster Blue-Veil, Emergency Evacuation Reserve, and a list of freight clients so dead their grandchildren would be dust. If you arrive as yourself, the refinery will thank you for your material contribution and assign output to pending obligations. Then it will eject you from the intake ring with polite force.

So yes, I traffic in names.

Not false names. Never false. False breaks too quickly.

I traffic in broken names, partial names, names with enough checksum left in them to make old systems nod and look away. Half an access braid from a civic quartermaster. A depot seal copied from a heat-bleached crate. A route waiver signed by an office that no longer exists but whose key tree still propagates through three surviving ports.

You would be amazed how much of empire survives as formatting.

The girl who brought me the Blue-Veil ribbon thought it belonged to her mother. Maybe it did, in the same way an anchor belongs to the sea floor after the ship is gone. The ribbon had been sewn into a coat lining for luck. Under the stitching was a municipal evac credential, degraded but legible in machine bands. I cleaned it, rebuilt the broken edges, and paired it with a custody ledger from an abandoned intake school.

Sel Edge saw the packet and opened like a flower.

The first time that happens in front of someone, they always go a little religious. Doors that have ignored a whole century of shouting will slide aside for a whisper in the right dead language. Output rails will reroute. Packaging lines will relabel. A refinery that would not spare you a screw as a living scavenger will produce ten crates of hull patch reserve because, for fourteen minutes, it believes you are the lawful hand of a forgotten emergency.

That is not theft, in my opinion.

That is inheritance performed with better tools.

Still, I understand why the councils hate people like me. We reveal how much of their authority is theater draped over machine habits they do not control. A station charter means less to Sel Edge than a child's coat lining with the right municipal residue in it.

Sometimes, very late, I wonder whether the old world deserved to die if it made ownership this thin.

Then I remember the faces in a dry dock when the repair crates arrive.

Paper ghosts, dead offices, legal necromancy, whatever name people want to use for it: hulls stop leaking just the same.
