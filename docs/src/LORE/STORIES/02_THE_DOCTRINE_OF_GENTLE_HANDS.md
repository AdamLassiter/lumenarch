# The Doctrine of Gentle Hands

_An oral lesson recorded from Quartermaster Nera Vale of Spindle Mercy, with annotations by apprentice deckhand Pio Marr._

"You can laugh," Nera said, "so long as you laugh with both gloves on."

That was how the lesson began, because Pio had laughed. He had laughed at the brass plaques above the vent controls, each one worn smooth by three generations of thumbs.

`NAME THE CHAMBER.`

`WAKE THE GRID GENTLY.`

`DO NOT ARGUE WITH PRESSURE.`

He had asked if the station was run by engineers or grandmothers.

Nera did not answer immediately. She turned the lamp to full and showed him the vent manifold where the old metal puckered around a seam like scar tissue.

"This was Hush Bay," she said. "Once."

Pio looked down the corridor. "What is it now?"

"A wall. Because someone opened a hot chamber into a cold service trunk without equalizing and without warning the ring. Twenty-six dead, including the woman who trained me."

Pio stopped smiling.

Nera tapped the first plaque with one knuckle.

"Name the chamber. Why?"

He shrugged.

"Because if you say the chamber name out loud, the person on the other side has one last chance to hear you make a mistake. Because if there are two chambers with similar handles, your mouth catches what your hands miss. Because in smoke, in panic, in half-light, your voice is one more instrument."

She tapped the second plaque.

"Wake the grid gently. Why?"

"Because... because surges?"

"Because old lines lie. Because old capacitors remember dying. Because a dead station sometimes comes back angry if you ask everything from it in one breath. There used to be numbers for how fast, and charts for sequence order, and pretty little manuals. What we have now is this sentence, and the sentence is enough if you obey it."

She tapped the third plaque.

"Do not argue with pressure."

That one he understood without help.

Nera saw it in his face and softened a little.

"You think ritual is what happens after knowledge is lost. Sometimes it is what knowledge turns into when people are too tired, too hungry, or too frightened to keep teaching the whole machine every time."

Pio ran his glove over the lettering.

"So this was all just procedure once?"

"Just procedure?" She snorted. "Boy, procedure is civilization with the poetry squeezed out. Then the world got smaller and meaner and the poetry seeped back in through the cracks."

Later, after the lesson, Pio copied the plaques into his notebook and added his own comments in the margin.

`NAME THE CHAMBER.`  
_Embarrassing until you watch an elder catch your mistake before the wheel turns._

`WAKE THE GRID GENTLY.`  
_Not metaphorical._

`DO NOT ARGUE WITH PRESSURE.`  
_Still not metaphorical._

Below that, in smaller writing, he added what Nera had said when she thought he was no longer listening:

"We did not keep the rites because we forgot the reasons. We kept them because forgetting was faster than teaching, and the dead do not forgive shortcuts."

Years later, when Pio was the one teaching children on Spindle Mercy, he found himself repeating the lines in the same dry voice Nera had used. The children rolled their eyes exactly as he had.

He made them say the chamber names aloud anyway.
