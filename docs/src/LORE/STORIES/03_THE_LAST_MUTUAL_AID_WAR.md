# The Last Mutual Aid War

_Compiled from two public commemorations and one private letter never sent._

## I. Memorial Address at Gault Ring

We remember the war forced upon us by misers and machine-hoarders.

We remember that aid without limits is not aid but surrender. We remember that the first duty of a station is to its own air, its own children, its own hull. When Kalden Spoke withheld recovery computers and traffic predictors from joint use, they did so not as stewards but as future tyrants. They meant to own the routes by owning the only minds that could manage them.

We did not begin the severance. We enforced it.

Let this ring teach what the dead paid to learn: a tool too powerful for common use is a form of blackmail, even before it is pointed like a weapon.

## II. Founding Day Recitation at Kalden Spoke

We remember the famine of waiting.

We remember begging Gault Ring to release reserve processors so the lanes could be synchronized and the refinery queues normalized, and we remember their answer: that no machine should stand above neighborly virtue. We remember their virtue while freighters missed dock, while habs browned out, while hand-kept ledgers grew too slow to feed anyone.

We did not choose separation because we loved command. We chose it because sermons do not route cargo.

Let this spoke teach what the dead paid to learn: fear of the old world is not wisdom if it condemns the living to smaller graves.

## III. Unsent Letter, found in a sealed locker between the two stations

Mira,

By the time you read this, if you ever do, both councils will have turned our argument into ancestry.

I sat in the sixth negotiation and watched sensible people become symbols in real time. Gault delegates entered already determined to be the last honest poor. Kalden delegates entered already determined to be the first competent rulers. Each side needed the other to behave badly so their own fear would feel like principle.

You asked me once what the war was about.

It was about three processors.

No historian will write it that way because it sounds too small, but it was three intact supervisory cores and the question of whether they should be distributed across the mutual aid chain or concentrated where they would do the most immediate good. Kalden had the technicians and the route urgency. Gault had the population and the memory of what centralized machine authority had already done to the old world.

Both were right.

Both were afraid.

Then a convoy spoiled in queue because manual clearance fell behind by fourteen hours. Then Gault militia boarded a Kalden courier carrying copied boot architecture. Then Kalden started escorting its freight with armed hulls. After that everyone could say war had become inevitable, which is a comforting lie people tell after they have made inevitability with their own hands.

The part I cannot forgive is how quickly the dead improved the speeches.

A week after the breach, boys who had never touched a scheduler were talking about destiny. Women who had once shared repair stock over tea were using words like contamination and stewardship in tones borrowed from funeral liturgy. By the second month, each side had already begun editing its own beginning.

If this letter never reaches you, perhaps it is because someone sensible destroyed it. No station likes records that suggest its founding wound was ordinary.

But if it does reach you, keep one fact alive for me:

the last mutual aid war was not fought between generosity and greed, nor between courage and cowardice.

It was fought between two groups of tired survivors, each holding a different piece of the same broken truth, each too frightened to let the other carry it.

- Ilen
