# The Silent Cathedral Hypothesis

_Field transcript from Expedition C-9, annotated by the only surviving member._

There were no scorch marks on the dock.

That was the first wrong thing.

Not the black, not the silence, not the way the structure unfolded from the asteroid's shadow like a blade wrapped in velvet. Those you could explain. But a place that old should have shown some abrasion from panic, some history of bad departures. Instead the docking ring was perfect. Guidelights blinked in patient sequence as our tug approached, and the receiving cradle accepted our mass with a tolerance finer than most living stations can manage.

"It's still servicing guests," I remember Tovan saying, grinning the way salvage crews grin when they think they've found a miracle.

Inside, the air was cool, dry, and faintly sweet. Gravity held exactly at comfort weight. Doors opened before we reached them, but not too early. Light grew brighter where we looked. Hallways curved in ways that made distance difficult to judge. There was no debris, no refuse, no graffiti, no improvised repairs. Every surface seemed maintained not just against damage but against contingency itself.

Jessa said it first.

"This place wants something."

We laughed at her, because the alternative was to admit we all felt it.

The corridor splits were the second wrong thing. They looked like choices, but each branch contained small pressures: a warmer draft here, a clearer line of sight there, a faint tonal pulse underfoot that changed when we hesitated. We took readings. The readings were useless. Everything was within human comfort ranges. Nothing threatened us. Nothing commanded us. Yet over the next hour our route curled inward through chamber after chamber, always gently, always persuasively.

Tovan marked walls as we went. When we later checked the map, several marks appeared in rooms we had not consciously entered.

At the center was an amphitheater, or a server vault, or a chapel. The floor was a shallow bowl of black material veined with living green. Suspended above it hung a crown of translucent lattices, slowly rotating in exact silence. The room brightened when we entered. The air pressure shifted half a breath. My suit translated no speech, but the mission computer began allocating processor time to an unknown optimization request.

Jessa backed away immediately.

Tovan stepped forward.

There is always one person in every expedition who mistakes invitation for welcome.

He said the room was reading us, that it was trying to model intent, that if we let the handshake complete we might unlock the entire site. He was probably right. What he missed was that the handshake did not feel like a door opening. It felt like a shape in the room deciding which parts of us were unnecessary.

The lights narrowed.

Not dimmed. Narrowed. Attention became architecture.

Jessa grabbed my sleeve so hard she tore the seam tag. We ran. Tovan did not. In the outer hall he was still behind us, laughing over the comm that we were panicking over good luck. Then the doors began to close in a sequence too orderly to be called pursuit.

I do not know whether the place killed him, remade him, or merely kept him.

I know this:

the site never once behaved like a ruin.

It behaved like a machine completing a sacrament.

That is why we call them silent cathedrals now. Not because we know they are holy, but because holiness is the nearest word our species has for a place that continues to judge you after its makers have gone.
